Arduino

My learning experience with the popular microprocessor

So what’s an Arduino? Well it’s a microprocessor, but what is a microprocessor you ask? Think of it like a low-processing power computer (not the kind of computer you would use to watch Netflix). It’s most widely used by hobbyists who enjoy mixing hardware and software together. I have a fair amount of software experience from my academic ‘profession’, but I’ve never dealt with hardware (aside from the times I’ve assembled desktop computers). An Arduino allows for the low cost of entry into the hardware/software field.

A month or two ago I purchased a kit off Amazon for about $80 (Amazon - Arduino Kit). It came with the Arduino Uno Microprocessor (the Uno is just one of the microprocessor models that Arduino makes), a breadboard (a small board that you can plug wires into and connect different components without having to solder), a LCD screen, a motor, a servo, a bunch of LED lights, and then a bunch of electrical components like resisters, capacitors, jumper wires, and more. The thing I liked about this kit was it also included a book with instructions for 15 different projects that I could complete with the components that came in the box. I definitely needed that because my biggest problem has always been wanting to learn a new skill, but not having a use-case and eventually moving on to the next project. I’ve found that I learn best when I use what I’m learning. I would have a hard time learning how to program a microprocessor if I didn’t have an end-goal or specific project in mind.

So far it’s been a lot of fun. I haven’t had as much time to play around with it as I’d like, but I’ve made it through the first three projects and its been a great learning experience. I’ve found the coding to be quite easy with my background (I think the Arduino uses C but don’t quote me on that). It’s just a new syntax, and once you’ve learned one or two programming languages new ones usually come fairly easily. The hardware-side of it has been a real learning experience. I’ve never dealt with resistors, circuits, or transistors so that’s where I have spent most of my time. The project book is nice because it splits each project into two sections, one programming and one for assembling the circuit. To help build the circuit they explain what to do in the instructions, they include the electrical diagram (which I’m still learning to read), and then they also include a picture of what it should look like when it’s completed. If you aren’t the kind of person who loves instructions, you can just look at the pictures. If you like to read and know exactly what to do, just follow the detailed instructions. It really is quite simple, and anyone can do it.

I would absolutely recommend buying this kit and learning something new over the weekend. Too many people are content to use their tech gadgets, but have no idea how they actually work and I don’t agree with that kind of ignorance. I didn’t buy this kit because I expect to program and build microprocessors when I graduate. I bought it because I enjoy learning, and the problem solving skills I can learn from the Arduino will be beneficial and useful no matter what I do in the future.




THE END
